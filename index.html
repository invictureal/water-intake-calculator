How your daily water intake is calculated

This water intake counter starts from a baseline of about 33 ml per kilogram of body weight, then layers on adjustments for activity level (up to +1000 ml for intense exercise), weather (up to +750 ml in heat), and health status (pregnancy, breastfeeding, or fever all raise fluid needs). Height contributes a small body-surface-area correction using the Mosteller formula, since larger-framed bodies lose slightly more fluid through skin and breath at the same weight. The result is then converted into water intake per day figures you can actually use — liters, glasses, and bottles. This is a general wellness estimate, not medical advice — people managing kidney, heart, or liver conditions should follow fluid guidance from their doctor.

Frequently asked questions

How much water should I drink a day?

Most adults need roughly 30–35 ml per kilogram of body weight daily, adjusted upward for activity, heat, pregnancy, breastfeeding, or illness. Use the calculator above for a number tailored to you.

Does coffee or tea count toward water intake?

Yes, in moderation. Caffeinated drinks still contribute fluid, though plain water remains the best default choice for daily hydration.

Is this calculator a substitute for medical advice?

No. It provides a general wellness estimate. Anyone with a kidney, heart, or liver condition should follow the fluid guidance given by their doctor instead.

Why does the calculator ask for my height?

Height is combined with weight to estimate body surface area, which has a small but real effect on fluid loss through skin and breathing. It's a minor adjustment compared to activity and weather.

How many glasses of water is that per day?

The calculator converts your target into glasses, bottles, and cups automatically, using a standard 250 ml glass. Most adults land between 8 and 12 glasses a day depending on weight, activity, and weather.
